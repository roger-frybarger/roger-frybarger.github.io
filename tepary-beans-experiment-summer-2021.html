Roger's Tepary Bean Experiment of 2021

Hello internetz, apparently a bunch of you are curious about how I managed to grow a bunch of beans in the hot sonoran desert summer without any soil amendments, fertilizer, or daily wattering. Below I will attempt to briefly explain how I accomplished this feat along with some things I learned along the way:


So, this whole adventure started early in the summer of 2021 as a quest to obtain shade for the west wall of our house. I was doing research on the internet about what kinds of climbing/vining plants would be able to survive our intense summers. I did find several options in that regard, but none that really perked my interest. I was also hesatant to regularly water something for the sole purpose of obtaining shade. Eventually after persisting at this search for quite some time, I stumbled upon Tepary beans as an option. These really facinated me as I had always wondered how the native peoples of this area had survived in such a harsh environment for thousands of years. Researching Tepary beans helped answer those questions, and inspire me to try growing them for myself.

Ultimately I decided to try out Tepary beans as an experiment first, and evaluate their feasibility as temporary shade plants for the west wall. I started by purchasing seeds for Yellow Tepary from nativeseeds.org. Once they arrived, I planted 11 of them in a rabbit-protected area arround July 10th 2021. Shortly thereafter I added a 5.5 foot (168 cm) tall trelis for them to climb up. I planted each seed about an inch deep, which I felt was about right given how quickly our soil dried out. I also ensured there was a pronounced water basin arround the entire planting area to hold the water by the seeds. I chose a planting area that recieved some afternoon shade as I was suspicious of the calimed heat tollerance on the seed packet. However, I would feel comfortable planting these in full sun next time. Initially, I watered these seeds every evening so that they would sprout. However, after that initial sprouting period, I transitioned to watering roughly every 4 days unless it rained. I did not add any organic mater, mulch, fertilizer, or poting soil before planting as I wanted to determine whether our native soil was fit for growing these beans.

As the experiment progressed I learned many things. The trelis did prove useful and by August 1st they had already climbed about 2.5 feet (76 cm) up it. Unfortunetly, I was also starting to have disease problems at this point and had to remove several leaves to controll some type of white fungi. After researching the issue I decided to switch to botom watering to avoid spreading the disease any further. Keeping the plant leaves dry definetly helped and allowed the experiment to continue. In hindsight, I also should have planted the seeds further apart. I didn't know how big the plants would get, and planted them about 2 inches apart. This was too close together. I should have planted them about 5 or 6 inches apart for beter airflow between the plants. This would have helped reduce disease spread as well.

By August 25th our monsoon season was in full swing and we had already recieved almost 10 inches of rain. This was great as I was able to get by without watering for weeks at times. Unfortunetly it also brought in every plant pest one could possibly imagine. This amount of summer rainfal is somewhat unusual for this area, and as such, the native plants don't typically need to handle the amount of pest preasure that we recieved this summer. This meant that these tepary beans were not fully prepared to handle the abundance of bugs. Most of the bugs were however repeled by the natural defences of the tepary beans. The one bug that did manage to get past these defences was the lace bug, which proceeded to suck the plant juces, bring in more diseases, and multiply quickly. To combat this unwelcome invador I researched Garlic spray on the internet and experemented with various mixtures. At first I used an overly concentrated mixture of dish soap and garlic concentrate, which suceeded in repelling the bugs, but also burned the bean leaves. Eventually I found an effective spray recipe that did not burn the leaves, but I wish I had done this research before I needed the spray. While I was fiddling about with the various garlic spray experiments the bugs did a fair amount of damage to the plants.

By September 12th I had perfected the recipe for the garlic spray and was checking the beans each evening and applying the spray as needed. Next summer I will pre-mix the spray and have it ready to deploy imediately as soon as I start seeing those bugs. On a brighter note, the beans had made it up to the top of the trelis by August 25th, and had been flowering and setting beans for about two weeks. The bean pods are covered with small hairs which tend to disuade preadators. They are also well camouflaged amongst the folage, so it is hard to tell how many beans are forming until they start to fill in and change color. The plants are self-polinated, but they seemed to attract various polinators as well.

By September 23rd the harvest had officially started and I started checking the plants daily and scisoring off any beans that rattled. Between September 23rd and October 4th I was usually able to pick an entire handfull of beans each day, which I then shelled and preserved for planting next year or perhaps cooking. In all, this experiment has produced over 127 grams of beans from those 11 seeds, which I am positively thrilled about! I will definitely be growing more of these next summer and I hope that you will be as well! To further help you in that process here are some other various things that I learned from this experement:

1. These beans have decended from a species of bean that was native to the southwest United States and northern Mexico long before the Columbian exchange. They were cultivated for thousands of years by the native peoples of the area and were selectively bread to be larger and more productive. Thus, they retain the drought and heat tollerance of their wild ancestors while also being molded by human hands into what was a staple food source.

2. Because these beans orriginally decended from desert plants they retain certain characteristics of those wild relatives. Notable amongst these characteristics are the ability to adjust their leaves in such a way that the intense sunlight and heat does not burn them. They do this by tilting their leaves almost paralel to the rays of the sun so that the sunlight is hitting them at a significant angle, thus making it less intense on any given point on the leaf. Also notable is the tendancy for the beans to split open once dry and throw their seeds out across the desert. This was a sucessful spreading strategy for the ancestors of this plant, but can be somewhat bothersome to those growing it today. This tendancy means that it is very important to regularly check the plants during the harvest season as dry beans can't simply be left on the plant until they all mature. If you do this, the beans will end up splitting open and flinging the seeds everywhere.

3. I have heard varying points of view from various diferent sources regarding watering. Some sources indicate that these beans can be grown on the sonoran desert on monsoon rains alone, while others report that they still produced well in wetter areas of the country. I personally feel that both of these viewpoints may be partially correct. All plants need regular water to start from seed. However, beyond that, I personally only watered mine during dry periods between monsoon rains. When I did water I deep watered rather than just weting the surface. I do know that they need some amount of drought stress to produce at all, so if you are trying to grow them in a wet area, you may have trouble if they are not getting that drought stress.


In summary, I have been very impressed with the tenacity of these bean plants and will definetly be growing them again next summer. I am not sure they would be applicable for use as a summer shade structure for our west facing wall, but as things tured out I may not really need the shade that badly after all, so I still have yet to explore that use.

I feel that this result is very much in line with what I have experienced in my own experiment.
